Academic vocabulary · what we do for ELLs

Six terms we will use today, frontloaded for you. This is the move you will make for your own students.

Term 01

Multilingual learner

A student who uses more than one language in daily life. The asset based term for what California historically called English Learner. We use both interchangeably.

Term 02

Scaffold

A temporary support that lets a student access grade level work they could not yet do on their own. Removed when no longer needed. Examples: sentence frames, visuals, partner protocols.

Term 03

Cognate

A word that shares a root and meaning across two languages. Family and familia. Photosynthesis and fotosíntesis. Powerful bridges between L1 and L2 for many ELLs.

Term 04

Objective

A specific, observable goal for what students will know or do by the end of a lesson. In ELL pedagogy, paired: a content objective AND a language objective, posted in student facing language.

Term 05

Formative assessment

A check for understanding during the lesson, not after, used to adjust teaching in real time. For ELLs, it works best in modalities (point to, draw, label) not just oral questions.

Term 06

Academic language

The vocabulary, syntax, and discourse used in school. Different from conversational English. Must be taught explicitly because it is rarely picked up by exposure alone.

The frame we are proposing
01

An English learner is a student learning English while learning everything else in English. The cognitive load is doubled, and constant.

02

The home language (L1, the first or native language) is not in the way. It is the foundation that makes the new language (L2, the second language being acquired) possible (Cummins, 2000).

Multilingual education is not remediation. It is an asset based design that develops content and language together, for the student, in the student's full linguistic life.

03

Equity here is not a posture. It is grade level access, scaffolded with intent, every period, every day.

What we are after

Four goals of multilingual education in K-12.

Goal 01

Access

Grade level content with scaffolding. Never below level alternatives that lower the ceiling for the student.

Goal 02

Identity

Affirming home language and culture as assets, not deficits to overcome on the way to English.

Goal 03

Proficiency

Both content mastery and academic English, developed in parallel. Not English first, then everything else.

Goal 04

Belonging

A classroom where every linguistic background is heard, named, and built into the lesson, not erased.

Each goal · what it asks of you

The four goals, with a concrete teacher move for each.

The goals are abstractions until they live in your planning. Each one names a specific design decision you make before the lesson begins, and each one maps to a UDL principle.

  1. 01

    Access · grade level content, fully scaffolded. The most common error is to substitute a lower level text for an ELL. Instead, keep the grade level text and pair it with a graphic organizer, a glossary of pre taught terms, sentence frames for response, and a partner protocol. The ceiling stays high, the path becomes climbable. (UDL · Representation)

  2. 02

    Identity · home language as foundation. Use cognates explicitly (photosynthesis and fotosíntesis). Invite students to write or discuss in their L1 and bridge to English. Build student linguistic histories into the year, every student's languages on the wall. (UDL · Engagement and Identity)

  3. 03

    Proficiency · content and language, in parallel. Every lesson plan has two objectives, posted in student facing language. A content goal (students will compare two ecosystems) and a language goal (students will use comparison language: both, however, while) in writing and discussion. Both get taught. Both get assessed. (UDL · Action and Expression)

  4. 04

    Belonging · every voice has a designed place. Use language routines that ensure equity of voice (we will define these later in this section). Honor multilingualism out loud. Make being multilingual visibly more interesting than being monolingual. (UDL · Engagement)

Where the work begins

Two students. Same lesson. Different cognitive labor.

The native English receiver · learning in their L1

Decoding content in their first language.

  • Decoding the content alone
  • Drawing on background knowledge with familiar vocabulary
  • Following classroom norms in their L1 culture
  • Making meaning in their dominant language
  • Speaking up without translating first
The English learner · learning in their L2

Decoding content in a language still being acquired.

  • Decoding the content AND the language carrying it
  • Translating new vocabulary in real time
  • Following classroom norms across cultures
  • Making meaning while their L1 sits unused and unhonored
  • Often understanding more than they can produce

Why this is genuinely hard

Five frameworks for what English learners are doing all day.

None of these is an excuse. They are the conditions of the work, and once you see them, your planning changes.

  1. 01

    Decode while building meaning. Conversational English, what Cummins calls BICS (Basic Interpersonal Communication Skills), takes 1 to 2 years to develop. Academic English, CALP (Cognitive Academic Language Proficiency), takes 5 to 7 years. Most ELLs sit in content classes well before they reach CALP (Cummins, 1980; 2000).

  2. 02

    Filter affect from input. Krashen's Affective Filter Hypothesis. When anxiety is high or self confidence is low, comprehensible input cannot become acquired language (Krashen, 1982). Belonging is not soft. It is mechanically necessary.

  3. 03

    Carry receptive ahead of expressive. Many ELLs have a silent period. They understand far more than they can say. Calling on them cold is not a check for understanding. It is a check for fluency they do not yet have.

  4. 04

    Bridge background knowledge. Course materials assume cultural references, idioms, and prior content the student may never have encountered. The bridge is not optional, and it is the teacher's job to build it.

  5. 05

    Hold identity steady in a non native register. Operating all day in a second language is exhausting. Identity, confidence, and belonging are not extras, they are prerequisites for input to land.

The teacher moves that matter

Five non negotiables in planning when ELLs are in the room.

These show up in every research based framework. SIOP, WIDA (World Class Instructional Design and Assessment), and UDL (Universal Design for Learning, the framework you will live in throughout this credential program).

  1. 01

    Write language objectives alongside content objectives. What students will say, read, write, and do in English, named explicitly, on the board, in student facing language. This is UDL's Engagement principle in action. Students know the why and the what before they begin. (UDL · Engagement)

  2. 02

    Frontload key vocabulary. Six to eight tier 2 and tier 3 words, taught with images, gestures, and home language cognates before reading. Not when students stumble during it. We will define the tiers on the next slide. (UDL · Representation)

  3. 03

    Pair every input with a visual. A photo, a labeled diagram, a graphic organizer. Cognitive load drops when the eye carries half the meaning the language is asking the ear to carry alone. UDL calls this multiple means of representation. (UDL · Representation)

  4. 04

    Build language routines into every lesson. Predictable structures like Think Pair Share, accountable talk stems, and conversation lines. The student needs to produce language to acquire it, and a routine gives them a scaffold to feel safe doing it. (UDL · Engagement and Action)

  5. 05

    Check for understanding in modalities, not just oral questions. Point to, draw, label, gesture, write, sort. UDL's multiple means of action and expression. Let the student show you what they know in their strongest channel. (UDL · Action and Expression)

Vocabulary you frontload

Tier 1, Tier 2, and Tier 3, in plain language.

A framework from Beck, McKeown, and Kucan (Bringing Words to Life, 2002), now standard across literacy and ELL pedagogy. Knowing which tier a word belongs to tells you how to teach it.

  1. 01

    Tier 1 · everyday words. Words native English speakers learn from speech and family life: house, run, happy, water, walk. ELLs may need direct instruction in these because they were not in their L1 environment, but native speakers do not. Use pictures, gestures, and L1 cognates.

  2. 02

    Tier 2 · high frequency academic words across content areas. Words like analyze, contrast, infer, explain, summarize, evidence. These power academic English across science, history, and ELA. They are the highest yield words to frontload, because every grade level test and discussion uses them. Prioritize 6 to 8 of these per unit.

  3. 03

    Tier 3 · domain specific technical terms. Words tied to one content area: photosynthesis, isotope, denominator, cuneiform, sonnet. Often best taught in context with images, models, or demonstrations. Less frequent than Tier 2 but essential to the discipline.

  4. 04

    The teacher move. When you plan a unit, identify 6 to 8 Tier 2 words and 4 to 6 Tier 3 words. Frontload them with visuals and cognates before the reading. Bring them back in language routines throughout the lesson. (UDL · Representation, Engagement)

Language routines · what they are

Language routines, defined.

A language routine is a predictable, repeatable structure for student talk that scaffolds language production while practicing content. Routines turn a content lesson into oral language practice without losing the content. Drawn from Zwiers and Crawford, Academic Conversations (2011) and Stanford's Understanding Language initiative.

  1. 01

    Why they work. Routines reduce the cognitive load of figuring out how to talk so the student can focus on what to say. The structure is the scaffold. The structure also creates equity of voice, because every student gets a turn, not just the confident ones.

  2. 02

    Think Pair Share. Pose a question, give 30 seconds of silent think time, students discuss with one partner for one to two minutes, then a few share with the class. Builds in processing time, low risk practice, and accountability. The simplest, highest yield routine in any classroom.

  3. 03

    Accountable talk stems. Sentence frames posted on the wall: I agree with ___ because, my evidence is, I want to add to that, can you say more about, a counter example would be. Students borrow the language of academic discussion until they own it.

  4. 04

    Conversation lines (also called inside outside circles). Two lines face each other. Students discuss a prompt for 90 seconds with the partner across from them, then one line shifts and they discuss with a new partner. Repeats six to eight times. Builds fluency through repetition. Powerful for ELLs.

  5. 05

    QSSSA (Question, Signal, Stem, Share, Assess). A Seidlitz protocol. Pose a question, students signal when they have an answer, you give them a sentence stem, they share with a partner, you assess by listening in. Useful when you want every student to commit to an answer before sharing. (Seidlitz and Perryman, 7 Steps to a Language Rich Interactive Classroom, 2022)

UDL · the framework you will live in

Universal Design for Learning. Three principles, one design philosophy.

Developed by CAST (Center for Applied Special Technology), now in version 3.0 (released 2024). UDL is built on the science of how brains learn. It anticipates learner variability, including English learners, from the design phase, not as an after the fact accommodation.

  1. 01

    Engagement · the WHY of learning. Multiple ways to spark interest, sustain effort, and self regulate. For ELLs: low stakes participation paths through language routines, identity affirming content choices, predictable routines that reduce anxiety.

  2. 02

    Representation · the WHAT of learning. Multiple ways to perceive content, language, and symbols. For ELLs: visuals, gestures, audio versions, cognates, frontloaded Tier 2 and Tier 3 vocabulary, simplified syntax that preserves meaning rather than dilutes it.

  3. 03

    Action and Expression · the HOW of learning. Multiple ways for students to physically act, express what they know, and direct their own work. For ELLs: draw it, label it, gesture it, write it, speak it. Show mastery in your strongest modality.

  4. 04

    Identity and variability · the WHO of learning. UDL 3.0's key emphasis. Every learner brings multiple, intersecting identities. For ELLs, multilingual identity is an asset to design around, not a barrier to overcome. Source · udlguidelines.cast.org.

UDL × ELL · in practice

How UDL gives English learners grade level access.

Six UDL scaffolds you can use in your first lesson plan. Each removes a specific barrier ELLs face in content classrooms, without lowering the academic ceiling. Notice how language routines and Tier 2 vocabulary keep showing up, the frameworks reinforce one another.

  1. 01

    Offer the same text in multiple representations. Pair the textbook passage with a visual organizer, an audio version, and a Tier 2 vocabulary preview. Same grade level content, multiple entry points. (Representation)

  2. 02

    Vary how students show understanding. Let them draw the water cycle, label a diagram, narrate it, or write it. The grade level concept is the same, the modality flexes to the learner. (Action and Expression)

  3. 03

    Build choice into the assignment. Choice of topic, partner, or product within a single learning goal. Identity rises, engagement rises, cognitive load drops. (Engagement)

  4. 04

    Pre teach academic vocabulary with cognates and L1 equivalents. ELLs bridge from L1 to L2 instead of starting from zero. The home language is a scaffold, not noise. (Representation and Identity)

  5. 05

    Use a language routine in every lesson. Think Pair Share, conversation lines, accountable talk stems. The routine is the scaffold for both engagement and oral production. (Engagement and Action)

  6. 06

    Make every objective student facing. Content goal AND language goal posted in plain language, alongside a model of mastery. Reduces guesswork for everyone, especially ELLs whose anxiety spikes when expectations are vague. (Engagement and Action)

Why SIOP exists
01

Born from a study (1996 to 2003) funded by CREDE (Center for Research on Education, Diversity, and Excellence), asking a deceptively simple question. What does an effective sheltered lesson actually look like in practice?

02

Field tested with middle school teachers across four metros, two coasts. The protocol was iterated with the people doing the work (Echevarría, Vogt, and Short, 2000).

SIOP is not an ESL course. It is a planning and observation protocol for any content teacher whose classroom includes English learners, designed to make grade level content comprehensible without watering it down.

03

The point is observable practice. Not what the teacher intends. What the teacher does, and what the student therefore experiences.

Same content, different lesson

What changes when SIOP and UDL enter the room.

Without SIOP or UDL

A typical lesson, ELLs treated as a footnote.

  • One objective on the board, content only "Today we will study the causes of the Civil War."
  • Vocabulary defined on the fly when students stumble "Secession means leaving" said in passing as the lecture moves on.
  • Teacher talk dominates, ELL comprehension assumed 15 minute lecture, then "any questions?" silence taken as understanding.
  • Practice equals silent independent worksheet Read the chapter, answer the questions at the end. ELLs work alone.
  • Assessment equals end of unit test in English Multiple choice and short answer in dense academic prose, no scaffolds.
With SIOP and UDL

The same content, designed for the students in the room.

  • Two objectives, content AND language, posted student facing "I can explain three causes of the Civil War, using the words secession, abolition, federalism in writing and discussion."
  • Six Tier 2 words frontloaded with images, gestures, and cognates Word wall up Day 1: secede / secesión, abolish / abolir, with photos and student gestures.
  • Teacher talk paced and paired with visuals, comprehension checked in three modalities 5 minute lecture, then "thumbs up if you can name one cause, point to the map, sketch the dividing line."
  • Practice equals language routine, hands on application, and writing Conversation lines: each student explains one cause to four different partners, then writes a paragraph using stems.
  • Exit ticket in students' strongest modality, with sentence frames "The most important cause was ___ because ___" or label the map, or record a 30 second voice memo.

What this looks like

Five SIOP and UDL moves you can use in your first lesson plan.

Each move belongs to a SIOP component, leans on a UDL principle, and uses a specific language routine. The frameworks reinforce one another.

  1. 01

    Frontload six Tier 2 words with image cards and student generated gestures before reading the textbook chapter. (SIOP Building Background · UDL Representation)A 5th grade science unit on ecosystems. Day 1, before any reading: post six word cards (habitat, predator, adapt, organism, ecosystem, decompose) with photos. Each student picks one and invents a gesture. Class repeats the gestures together. Words go on the wall for the unit.

  2. 02

    Pair every concept slide with a short visual. A photo, a labeled diagram, a flowchart, so language carries half the load. (SIOP Comprehensible Input · UDL Representation)A high school history lesson on the Industrial Revolution. Each slide pairs a one sentence claim with a photograph. Slide on factory conditions: photo of child laborers next to the sentence. Slide on urbanization: a population growth chart, not just a paragraph.

  3. 03

    Run a Think Pair Share or conversation lines routine with sentence frames. (SIOP Interaction · UDL Action and Expression)A 7th grade ELA class on a short story. Question on the board: "What is the protagonist's main internal conflict?" Frames: "I think the conflict is ___ because in the text it says ___." 30 seconds silent, two minutes pair, three voluntary shares.

  4. 04

    Choose one application task per lesson that requires students to talk, write, and produce something with their hands. What ELPAC is

The ELPAC, in five facts.

ELPAC stands for English Language Proficiency Assessments for California. It is the state's required test of English proficiency, administered by every California public school, governed by the CDE (California Department of Education), and aligned to the 2012 CA ELD Standards (English Language Development).

Principles · how vocabulary is acquired

Vocabulary is not a list. It is the bridge between a learner and a discipline.

The Vocabulary Acquisition Model rests on a small number of evidence based claims about how learners hold onto new words and use them to read, think, and speak in a content area (Estes & Mintz, 2016).

  1. 01

    Words are learned in webs, not lines. A new word lands only when it connects to a network of related words, images, examples, and the learner's prior experience. Isolated lists rarely transfer.

  2. 02

    Acquisition is incremental and repeated. Most words require multiple meaningful encounters across reading, talk, and writing before they enter active use. Frontloading once is not enough.

  3. 03

    Depth matters more than breadth. Knowing a word means knowing its meaning, its parts, its register, its cousins, and the contexts that hold it. Surface definitions rarely survive a content area assessment.

  4. 04

    Comprehension hinges on a small set of words. In any unit, a handful of words carry the conceptual load. Identify those, teach those, and the rest of the text becomes reachable.

  5. 05

    Talk drives ownership. Learners acquire a word when they use it aloud with peers and a knowledgeable adult, not when they hear it once and copy a definition.

Step 01 · in practice

Pre test knowledge of words critical to content.

Before instruction begins, find out which load bearing words students already own and which ones are new. The pre test is a planning tool for the teacher, not a graded event for the student (Estes & Mintz, 2016).

  1. 01

    Choose six to ten words that carry the unit's conceptual load. Tier 2 academic words (analyze, sequence, compare) and Tier 3 domain words (photosynthesis, sovereignty). Not every word in the text. The ones the unit cannot survive without.

  2. 02

    Use a self rating scale, not a definition quiz. Ask students to rate each word: I have never seen this word · I have seen it but cannot define it · I can use it in a sentence · I can teach it to a peer. Faster, more honest, and more diagnostic than asking for definitions.

  3. 03

    Pair the self rating with one short application item. A single sentence frame, a quick image to word match, or a brief synonym task. Lets you see whether self ratings match performance, particularly for ELLs who may underrate their own knowledge.

  4. 04

    Read the results as a planning map. Words rated never seen by most students go to direct instruction. Words rated can teach by most students get a quick reactivation, not a re teach. Words rated unevenly become candidates for peer talk in mixed rating pairs.

  5. 05

    Keep the pre test short. Five to ten minutes. Its job is to inform planning, not to absorb instructional time. Save the long assessment for the end of the unit.

Step 02 · in practice

Elaborate and discuss spellings and meanings.

Open the word up so students can see its parts. Spelling carries meaning. Talk surfaces understanding. This is the step that lets learners encounter a word from multiple angles before they have to perform with it (Estes & Mintz, 2016).

  1. 01

    Pull the word apart into morphemes. Prefix, root, suffix. Transportation becomes trans (across) plus port (carry) plus ation (the act of). Students who learn to decompose own dozens of related words.

  2. 02

    Surface the cognate, if there is one. About 30 to 40 percent of English academic vocabulary has a Spanish cognate. Analyze · analizar. Hypothesis · hipótesis. For ELLs whose L1 is Spanish, this is the fastest route into the word.

  3. 03

    Discuss the spelling, briefly and on purpose. Why the silent letter is there. Why the double consonant. Why the suffix changes form. Spelling discussions are not English class housekeeping. They are content instruction.

  4. 04

    Use talk routines to put the word in students' mouths. Turn and Talk with a sentence frame. Conversation Lines. Word Sort with a partner. The word has to leave the page before it can enter active vocabulary.

  5. 05

    Hold a brief example and non example exchange. Sovereignty is the authority a nation has over its own territory. Sovereignty is not the same as independence. Boundaries make meaning visible faster than definitions alone.

Step 03 · in practice

Directly teach the words on which comprehension will hinge.

For the small set of words that pre testing revealed are unknown and load bearing, plan explicit, repeated instruction. Direct teaching does not mean lecture. It means designed encounters, across modalities, multiple times (Estes & Mintz, 2016).

  1. 01

    Give a student friendly definition first. Not the dictionary. A sentence the student could repeat to a sibling. To analyze something means to take it apart so you can see how it works.

  2. 02

    Pair the definition with a visual. Photo, sketch, diagram, gesture. UDL Representation. The eye should carry half the meaning the ear is being asked to carry.

  3. 03

    Anchor the word in two contexts. One from within the discipline (scientists analyze data) and one from everyday life (you analyze a play in a basketball game). Two contexts beat one rich definition.

  4. 04

    Plan four to six exposures across the unit. Reading. Talk. Writing. Visual. Quick recall. Acquisition is incremental. One frontloaded encounter rarely transfers.

  5. 05

    Assess by use, not by definition. Can the student use the word correctly in a sentence about the unit. Can they recognize a non example. Definitions can be memorized without understanding. Use cannot.

Affix equivalencies · Spanish and English

Patterned equivalencies that let Spanish speaking ELLs unlock English academic vocabulary on first sight.

Equivalency 01

-tion · -ción

Education / educación. Nation / nación. Transportation / transportación. One of the most productive equivalencies in academic English. If a student can hear -ción, they can spell -tion.

Equivalency 02

-ty · -dad

Community / comunidad. Society / sociedad. Identity / identidad. The Spanish ending almost always signals the English noun ending. Strong for civics, social studies, and ELA vocabulary.

Equivalency 03

-ous · -oso

Famous / famoso. Generous / generoso. Curious / curioso. An adjective to adjective bridge. Students who own this can produce adjectives in English from Spanish roots.

Equivalency 04

-ly · -mente

Rapidly / rápidamente. Naturally / naturalmente. Generally / generalmente. An adverb to adverb bridge. The pattern itself can be taught in a single five minute mini lesson.

Equivalency 05

re- · re-

Review / revisar. Repeat / repetir. Reconstruct / reconstruir. The prefix carries the same meaning (again) in both languages. Direct transfer with no spelling adjustment.

Equivalency 06

-ive · -ivo / -iva

Active / activo. Creative / creativo. Productive / productivo. Spanish gender ending shifts, but the core adjective transfers. Worth surfacing every time it appears in a unit.

Assessment and differentiation

How you measure learning and how you reach the student who is not yet there.

Evaluating learning in the VAM

Assess by use, across modalities.

  • Compare the pre test to a parallel post test on the same word set
  • Ask for the word in writing inside a unit task, not on a vocabulary quiz
  • Listen for the word in student talk, with a simple tally sheet
  • Use a knowledge ladder self rating before and after the unit
  • Score generative use (using a root to decode a new word) as the highest mark
Meeting individual needs

Differentiate the entry point, not the destination.

  • Newcomers receive image first encounters and L1 cognate bridges
  • Developing ELLs receive sentence frames calibrated to current proficiency
  • Fluent English speakers extend with generative root challenges
  • Students with IEPs receive the same words, taught with fewer exposures per session and more repetitions across days
  • Every student is assessed against the same target words. The path differs, not the target.

What it looks like in the room

Potential teacher and student behaviors when the Vocabulary Acquisition Model is working.

Teacher behaviors

The teacher plans for, names, and revisits a small set of load bearing words.

  • Identifies six to ten critical words before the unit begins
  • Runs a brief, low stakes pre test in the first lesson
  • Frontloads with definition, visual, cognate, and example
  • Designs four to six exposures across the unit
  • Uses talk routines that put the word in every student's mouth
  • Assesses by use inside content tasks, not by isolated definitions
Student behaviors

Students use the words to think aloud, write, and decode new ones.

  • Self rate their knowledge honestly on the pre test and the ladder
  • Use sentence frames to put the new word in their own speech
  • Decompose words into roots and affixes when reading unknown vocabulary
  • Surface cognates aloud when a peer hits a hard word
  • Refer to the unit word wall during writing without prompting
  • Teach a peer the word using their own example

Why the model is worth your planning time

Four benefits of teaching vocabulary as a model, not as a list.

Benefit 01

Comprehension rises

When the load bearing words are owned, the text becomes reachable for the whole class. Reading scores tend to follow vocabulary depth, not vocabulary breadth.

Benefit 02

ELLs gain entry

Cognates, morphology, and visual anchored direct instruction give Multilingual Learners an honest route into grade level content. The ceiling stays high; the path becomes climbable.

Benefit 03

Knowledge generalizes

A root learned in one unit opens dozens of words in the next. Students do not just know more words; they become better at learning words, which is the durable outcome.

Benefit 04

Assessment is honest

Use based assessment captures what definition based assessment misses. Students who own the word use it; students who memorized it cannot. The model lets you tell the difference.

In class activity · 30 minutes · pairs

Activity 1 · Design a Step 01 pre test for a unit in your content area.

Pair with someone in or near your content area. Pick a unit you are likely to teach in your first year. Use the three steps below to build a Step 01 pre test you could give next Monday, then trade with another pair for feedback.

Step 01 · Choose
10 min

Pick the words on which comprehension will hinge.

Open the unit. Skim the anchor text. Build the short list together.

  1. List six to ten candidate words drawn from the anchor text.
  2. For each, mark Tier 2 (academic) or Tier 3 (domain specific).
  3. Strike any word the unit could survive without. Keep the load bearing ones.
  4. For Spanish cognate rich words, note the cognate. You will use it in Step 02.
  5. Defend the final list to your partner in one sentence per word.
Step 02 · Build
10 min

Draft a five minute pre test students can complete honestly.

The pre test is a planning tool. Honesty matters more than accuracy.

  1. Draft a self rating scale with four levels students can read on first sight.
  2. Pair the self rating with one short application item per word.
  3. Write the instructions in student facing language.
  4. Stress test on length: can a typical student finish in five to ten minutes?
  5. Plan how you will read the results to inform direct teaching.
Step 03 · Trade
10 min

Trade pre tests with another pair and revise.

A second pair of eyes will surface assumptions you cannot see.

  1. Take the pre test as if you were a Level 2 ELL in the class.
  2. Flag any item where the directions, sentence frame, or context would block a Level 2 student.
  3. Suggest one cognate or visual that would lower the entry barrier.
  4. Return the pre test with two strengths and two suggested revisions.
  5. Revise your own. Keep this draft. It is your first VAM artifact.

Basis · why the model works

An inductive approach. Students build the generalization themselves, from data the teacher curates.

The Integrative Model rests on the claim that conceptual understanding sticks when learners do the cognitive work of pattern finding themselves, rather than receiving a generalization to memorize (Estes & Mintz, 2016).

  1. 01

    Inductive reasoning is the engine. Students move from specific examples to broader patterns, then to generalizations. This mirrors how disciplinary thinking actually works. Scientists, historians, and economists reason inductively from evidence.

  2. 02

    The data set is the teacher's design move. What the teacher selects determines what students can see. A well chosen data set surfaces the target pattern without the teacher naming it.

  3. 03

    Talk is how patterns become visible. Pairs and small groups verbalize what they notice. Patterns that stay private rarely become principles.

  4. 04

    Generalizations are owned, not received. A student who hypothesizes and tests reaches the same conclusion as a student who is told, but the first one can use the conclusion in a new context.

  5. 05

    The model is content agnostic. It works for comparing organisms in biology, political parties in civics, geometric shapes in math, or characters in literature. The shape is the same; the data set changes.

The model · five steps

The Integrative Model in five steps. The first step is yours. The other four are theirs.

Step 01 is planning. The teacher selects the data set, identifies the target generalization, and decides how to scaffold the talk. Steps 02 through 05 are what students do, in order. Adapted from Estes and Mintz (2016).

  1. Step 01
    Teacher

    Plan the data set and the path

    Curate a data set that contains the target pattern. Identify what students should be able to generalize. Decide what they describe, compare, hypothesize, and conclude.

    EXAMPLE · 5TH GRADE WATER CYCLE

    Teacher curates eight phenomena (rain, evaporation, snow, transpiration, dew, fog, frost, condensation) and four attributes. Target: "Water cycles between Earth and sky as energy changes state."

  2. Step 02
    Students

    Describe, compare, find patterns

    Students examine the data set together. Name attributes. Compare across items. Identify similarities and differences. Surface candidate patterns.

    EXAMPLE · 5TH GRADE WATER CYCLE

    Pairs scan the matrix and call out patterns. "Evaporation and transpiration send water up. Rain and snow fall down." They group items by direction and by change of state.

  3. Step 03
    Students

    Explain similarities and differences

    Students propose explanations for the patterns they noticed. Why does this group share these features. Why does that one diverge. The explanation moves them from observation to reasoning.

    EXAMPLE · 5TH GRADE WATER CYCLE

    Pairs propose reasons. "Heat adds energy. Liquid becomes vapor. Water goes up." "Cold removes energy. Water comes down." Pattern: energy in equals up, energy out equals down.

  4. Step 04
    Students

    Hypothesize under new conditions

    What would happen if a variable changed. Students predict outcomes for cases not in the data set. This move tests the durability of their thinking.

    EXAMPLE · 5TH GRADE WATER CYCLE

    Teacher asks: "What would happen on a planet with no sun?" Pairs predict: "No evaporation, no clouds, no rain. The cycle stops." Then: "What if the air stayed warm year round?"

  5. Step 05
    Students

    Make broad generalizations

    Students state, in their own language, what the pattern teaches about the discipline. The generalization is the payoff. It carries into new units, new courses, and adult life.

    EXAMPLE · 5TH GRADE WATER CYCLE

    A student writes: "Water moves in a cycle between Earth and atmosphere, changing state as energy is added or removed." Teacher reveals the textbook version. Class compares wordings.

Generalizations students build themselves transfer to new contexts. Generalizations they are told tend to stay in the unit where they were taught.

Once you can plan one Integrative lesson, you can plan one in any unit, any subject, any grade. The shape is the same. The data set is the variable.

In class activity · 50 minutes · pairs

Activity 1 · Build a data set that contains a pattern you want students to find.

Pair up. Pick a unit from either partner's content area, or any unit you both find compelling. Cross discipline pairs are welcome. Curate a six to ten item data set, identify the target generalization, and draft the prompts students would respond to at each step. You will share your work aloud with the class at the end.

Step 01 · Choose
15 min

Pick the unit, the pattern, and the data set.

Decide together what students should generalize, then curate items that contain the pattern.

  1. Name the unit and the target generalization in one sentence.
  2. List six to ten items students will compare. Pick items whose comparison surfaces the pattern.
  3. Decide two to four attributes students will use to compare items.
  4. Build a matrix: items down the left, attributes across the top.
  5. Stress test the data set: would a fifth grader be able to see the pattern without being told?
Step 02 · Draft prompts
15 min

Write the student facing prompts for Steps 02 through 05.

Each prompt should put a specific cognitive move on the table without giving away the generalization.

  1. Step 02 prompt: what do you notice and wonder. Provide a sentence frame.
  2. Step 03 prompt: why might these group together. Provide a sentence frame.
  3. Step 04 what if prompt: what would change if a specific attribute were different.
  4. Step 05 prompt: write one sentence that captures the pattern in disciplinary language.
  5. Read each prompt aloud to your partner. Tighten any wording that gives the pattern away.
Step 03 · Share aloud
20 min

Share your data set and prompts with the class.

Each pair takes about three minutes to walk the rest of us through what you built.

  1. State the unit and the target generalization in one sentence.
  2. Walk through the data set items aloud. Name a few of the most telling examples.
  3. Explain your comparison attributes. Why these and not others.
  4. Demonstrate one pattern you would expect a student to surface in Step 02.
  5. Take one question from the class before the next pair shares.

Handout · for Activity 2 · lesson conversion lab

8th grade civics. Branches of the federal government. Fifty minutes.

Standard. 8.U.G.3 — explain the role and structure of the three branches of the U.S. federal government. Objective. Students will identify the three branches and explain how each one checks the others. Materials. Slide deck, copy of the worksheet, paragraph paper. The lesson below uses direct instruction throughout. Your job in Activity 2 is to convert it.

  1. 01

    Warm up · 5 min. Project pictures of the Capitol, the White House, and the Supreme Court building. Teacher says: "Today we will learn about the three branches of the federal government — legislative, executive, and judicial. Each one has different jobs and checks the others."

  2. 02

    Direct instruction · 20 min. Teacher presents slides covering each branch. "The legislative branch is Congress. It makes the laws. The executive branch is the President. The President enforces the laws. The judicial branch is the Supreme Court. It interprets the laws. Each branch can check the others. This is called checks and balances. For example, the President can veto a law. Congress can override the veto. The Supreme Court can declare a law unconstitutional."

  3. 03

    Guided practice · 10 min. Students fill in a worksheet table by copying the role, number of members, and term length for each branch directly from the slides. Teacher walks the room and helps students who fall behind.

  4. 04

    Independent practice · 10 min. Students write one paragraph answering: "Which branch is most powerful and why?" Teacher tells them there is no right answer, but to use the facts about each branch's powers from the slides.

  5. 05

    Close · 5 min. Teacher asks: "What are the three branches?" Students chorally answer. Teacher restates: "Legislative makes laws. Executive enforces. Judicial interprets. Checks and balances keeps any one branch from getting too powerful." Worksheet collected as exit ticket.
